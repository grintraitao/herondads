Herond · Business model

B2B2C advertising for Web3

Advertising priced per action, not selling personal data.

This document answers concretely: who we sell to, what we sell, when, where, how, and from what. It includes an advertiser's journey from need to payment, and how the money flows.

Updated 2026-06-25 · Diagnosis and roadmap are in the file "Diagnosis and 6-month plan"
01 · Framework

What we sell, to whom, where, from what

Who we sell to

Regional exchanges and Web3 projects (chains, dApps, GameFi, token projects) that need real users in Southeast Asia.

What we sell

Verified real actions: exchange signups, wallet connects, swaps, social tasks. Priced per action (CPA), not per impression.

Where we sell

On Herond surfaces: the Point home, the quests board, in-browsing context, and in the wallet.

From what

From Herond's own Southeast Asian user base plus the live quest engine. These are existing assets.

Why price per action, not per impression. Display prices (CPM) are very low in these markets, so they do not generate enough revenue. Web3 advertisers also tend to buy on results. Sources: HypeLab Crypto Ad Benchmarks 2026; a Blockchain-Ads case reporting roughly a 20 dollar average CPA in Southeast Asia.

02 · Client journey

From the need to the payment

Nine steps, from when an organization has a need to when the campaign is renewed.

1
An organization has a need

An exchange or a Web3 project needs real users in Southeast Asia, for example more signups, more people trying a dApp, or awareness around a token launch. Google and Meta restrict crypto advertising, so these projects lack a compliant channel.

2
They reach Herond

Through sales or referral. The reason to approach Herond is access to verified real users in Southeast Asia, rather than traffic that is hard to verify.

3
They become a client

Create an advertiser account, sign an agreement, and prepay budget into the account.

4
Set up a campaign

Choose the objective (signup, wallet connect, swap, social task), region, the human tier to target, the budget, and the CPA. Provide the ad creative or quest content.

5
Herond distributes

Push the offer catalog to user devices. The device matches by region and intent, then shows the quest or offer to the right users.

6
Users take action

Users opt in, complete the task, and receive points.

7
Verify and prevent fraud

Create a proof of humanity plus proof of the action. Hold the reward for a period to detect and claw back fraud.

8
Bill and report

Charge the CPA from the budget for each verified action. The report covers CPA, fraud rate, and retention of acquired users.

9
Optimize and renew

If acquired users prove to be high quality, the client increases budget and repeats.

03 · Worked example

A campaign by the Bitloka exchange

Context. The Bitloka exchange wants 500 signups with verified identity from real users in Indonesia within two weeks.

How it runs. Bitloka creates an advertiser account, deposits 10,000 dollars, sets a 20 dollar CPA per verified signup, and selects Indonesia and tier 1 verified humans. Herond places a quest, "Open a Bitloka account and verify identity," in the quests board, shown only to verified Indonesian users. Dwi sees the quest, completes it in a few minutes, receives 800 points worth about 8 dollars, and redeems airtime. Herond verifies the signup through the exchange's postback, holds for 24 hours against fraud, and only then charges 20 dollars to Bitloka.

Result. 500 signups equal 10,000 dollars of revenue, users receive about 4,000 dollars of airtime, and Herond books about 6,000 dollars gross before costs. In the report Bitloka sees a 22 percent day-7 retention, higher than its previous affiliate channel, so it increases budget.

The figures in this example are illustrative assumptions to show how it works and how money flows, not measured results.

06 · Anti-fraud, a critical condition

Proof of humanity in tiers

Tier 0. New

Not verified. Earns very little. Not counted as real

Tier 1. Verified

Device plus phone number. Real-user reach

Tier 2. Trusted

Plus on-chain wallet history. Higher-priced inventory

Core principle. Do not attach value to a cheap action. Rewards unlock only after the tier check, action verification, and a hold window for claw-back. This was the gap in April, when 590,000 bots flooded in because the reward unlocked simply on wallet creation. Proving real humanity is what creates value and differentiation for the model, provided the verification layer is built and validated.

08 · Pricing and methodology

Are these prices too high, and on what basis

Action typeValueMarket reference CPA
Follow, join socialLow0.2 to 1 dollar
Wallet connect, try a dAppMedium5 to 50 dollars (top campaigns about 2 to 3 dollars per verified wallet)
Exchange signup with identity verificationHigh10 to 30 dollars
Deposit, swap, transactionHighest20 to over 200 dollars
Methodology and benchmarks. These are market ranges, not invented numbers. Sources: HypeLab Crypto Ad Benchmarks 2026 (wallet connect 5 to 50 dollars, token purchase 20 to over 200 dollars, cost per verified wallet in top campaigns only 1.86 to 3.12 dollars); exchange affiliate programs (Business of Apps: 10 to 200 dollars per qualified user; Binance on a hybrid model of 10 to 15 dollars plus 20 to 40 percent revenue share); a Blockchain-Ads case reporting roughly a 20 dollar average CPA in Southeast Asia.
Are they too high. By the benchmarks above these ranges are not high relative to the market, and Southeast Asia tends to be cheaper than tier-1 markets. A reasonable approach is to anchor at the lower end of the range, price at or below existing channels, and compete on a low fraud rate and on retention, rather than on price.

Reward split

Users receive 30 to 50 percent of the CPA value, and Herond keeps the rest before costs.

Methodology. The reference point is Brave, which shares up to 70 percent with users for view ads. In a per-action model the user share is lower because the action already carries intrinsic value for the advertiser, and margin must be reserved for redemption costs. The final number should be settled through real testing, not fixed.

Points economy

The most important design decision. Separate hard points (cash-backed, created only from advertiser-funded actions paid in advance) from soft points (from engagement, capped). Minting points without backing and then allowing redemption causes inflation and collapse. The priority redemption sink is airtime and data.

Methodology for the 6 to 20 thousand dollars per month figure. Two sources combined. Low-price volume: about 16 thousand daily users, assuming 30 percent take one action per day, about 4,800 actions, at a net 0.03 to 0.10 dollars each, giving about 4 to 14 thousand dollars per month. High-price from exchanges: 1 to 2 percent of users per month complete a signup, about 160 to 320, at a net of about 12 dollars, giving about 2 to 4 thousand dollars. These are sizing estimates that require pilot validation. This is a base to prove willingness to pay and to support fundraising, not a large line of business yet.

Levers to grow: expand the user base, add a high-value vertical such as exchanges, and raise the verified share.
