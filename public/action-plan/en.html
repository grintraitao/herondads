Herond · Internal

Diagnosis and six-month plan

By the end you should see the problem and know what to do

A diagnosis of why Herond struggles to gain and keep users, with a six-month plan. For the product team and decision-makers.

Summary. Herond is weak at both ends: fewer new users arrive each month and almost all from a single source, Google Play, while most new users leave on day one. The common cause is that they never take a value action on day one. The document proposes focusing on exactly one thing: helping new users take one action right on day one (leaner onboarding, Herond Point without required sign-in) and acquiring the right high-quality markets. Revenue directions are parked in the Business Model file.
Updated 2026-06-30 · Data from BigQuery, 14 to 120 day windows depending on section

Over a million downloads and hundreds of thousands of wallets are mostly the April bot. Strip that out and the real scale is much smaller, so every goal must be measured with real users.

The story in one picture

Weak at both ends of the funnel, one root cause

Fewer new users arrive each month, and almost all from a single source, Google Play. Most leave on day one, and the common thread is they never use a feature. In contrast, those who take at least one action on day one, call it activate (bookmark, download content, or sign in), return at 45%, versus 28% for those who do nothing. The bottleneck is that onboarding currently blocks new users from taking that action.

The source is simple (mostly Google Play organic) but quality is uneven. On a cohort of 170,740 new users (last 120 days): D1 averages 22.8%, D7 7.1%, only 12,171 last to D7. Indonesia alone is 36% of that.

Data note. A cluster of installs are barely real: model 400 Smart has 1,241 installs but D7 only 0.4%, and old version 2.5.4 is similar. These are ultra-cheap phones opened once then dropped, not a pre-install deal. Just exclude this group when reading install numbers so they do not inflate the count.

98% of devices are budget Android (Redmi, Galaxy A, Infinix), retaining best on Xiaomi and Infinix (D7 around 8%), worst on Honor (4.5). Since the audience is low-spec devices, first measure whether performance (cold start, crash) is actually a reason for leaving before investing.

Language and OS. Indonesian retains best (D7 9.1), Vietnamese 7.6, English around 6.7; Spanish, Portuguese, Arabic are low (4 to 5.7), a sign of weak localization. OS is not a lever: newer devices D7 around 7.4, older 6.3 to 6.8, no churn cliff by version.

Takeaway. Acquisition is both shrinking and quality-skewed. The lever is not more installs but concentrating UA (user acquisition) on core Indonesia, testing scale in the high-quality small markets (Philippines, Egypt, Vietnam), and fixing localization for low-retention languages.

04 · Churn and the activation lever

Users leave very early and in large numbers; the common thread is they use no feature on day one. In contrast, those who take one value action on day one, call it day-0 activation (bookmark, download content, or sign in), stay at nearly double the rate. 06 · Problems

Five concrete problems

1. No active acquisition engine

88% of users come from Google Play organic, almost no paid or referral. When organic stalls, there is no lever to pull up.

2. Wrong users in the wallet area

Reward drops pulled in reward hunters and bots, used once then gone, never real scale.

3. The activation wall

About 74% of new users leave after one day, over half uninstall the same day. The common thread is no feature activation. There is a proven lever: day-0 activation lifts return from 28 to 45%.

4. The core is not in Web3

Those who stay do so for the browsing utility, not the wallet. The wallet is barely touched, W4 only 6 to 7%.

5. No revenue and weak measurement

No revenue model yet. Missing user_id and session_id on many events makes funnel and retention only a floor. Core value such as ad blocking and speed is barely tracked. The push channel for re-engagement sits unused.

Root cause. The core value is not enough to both pull users in and keep them. Resources go to the wallet and Web3, which the core group barely uses; while the browsing utility, the real value, has not been deepened.
07 · Optimizations to debate

Four changes aimed at acquisition, retention and Herond Point

Each proposal uses the same frame to avoid subjectivity: why (Why), who it targets (Who), which feature (Where), how (How), a measurable expectation, and effort. Priority is ranked by expectation over effort. All are hypotheses to A/B, not conclusions.

Priority by expectation over effort. Do the quick win first, removing the wallet button from the nav, then leaner onboarding, run acquisition-by-quality in parallel, and prepare the backend for login-free Herond Point.
1. Leaner onboarding: from three screens to oneHypothesis to test
Why
  • The funnel drops 51% at Welcome to Login and 53% at OTP.
  • First-run desktop only 22% set default.
  • The more onboarding steps, the more users fall off.
Who
New mobile users, the group losing 74% on day one.
Where
The onboarding and wallet-creation flow on first open: the Welcome, Login, OTP screens.
How
Collapse to one screen, build two variants with and without sign-in, A/B against current.
Expectation
Higher onboarding completion and day-0 activation, lifting D1 and D7.
Effort and priority
Medium effort (design plus client). High priority: hits the biggest drop point at moderate effort.
2. Remove the wallet button from the nav bar, hide it in the menuHypothesis to test
Why
  • The wallet is nearly inactive (~20 wallets sign a transaction per month, only 0.8% touch it).
  • It occupies prime nav space.
  • The retention core is browsing, not Web3.
Who
All users, especially those who stay for browsing.
Where
The bottom nav; the wallet moves into the menu.
How
Remove the wallet button from the nav, move it to the menu; the wallet still surfaces contextually in a dApp; put a value action like check-in in the freed slot.
Expectation
No retention loss (very few use the wallet) and space freed for an activation action.
Effort and priority
Low effort (a UI position change), low risk. Quick win, do it first.
3. Herond Point without required sign-in: check-in on the New TabHypothesis to test
Why
  • 0% of mobile users sign in, so earn value never reaches the largest group.
  • Day-0 activation lifts return from 28% to 45% but only 19% do it.
  • Sign-in is the biggest barrier to check-in.
Who
New mobile users who have not signed in.
Where
The New Tab (check-in and points), Herond Point, and the backend points ledger.
How
Anonymous earn per the server-authoritative design (anon-id, ledger, rule, rate-limit); merge on sign-in; redeem only after sign-in; cap pending points to prevent farming.
Expectation
Higher day-0 check-in rate, lifting day-0 activation and D1, D7.
Effort and priority
High effort (new backend and an anti-farm layer). Medium priority: high expectation but the backend must be prepared first.
4. Optimize new-user acquisition by qualityHypothesis to test
Why
  • New-user quality is very skewed: Indonesia is 36% of users retained to D7.
  • India has large install but D7 only 5.9%, burning installs with few lasting.
  • High-quality markets (Philippines, Egypt, Vietnam) are still small.
Who
The marketing team and the UA, ASO budget.
Where
The Google Play channel (ASO), localization by language, campaigns by country.
How
Concentrate UA and ASO on Indonesia plus Indonesian localization; test scaling Philippines, Egypt, Vietnam; re-localize low-retention languages; measure cold start and crash on budget Android before investing in performance.
Expectation
More users retained to D7 per dollar spent, higher new-user quality.
Effort and priority
Medium effort (marketing plus localization). Priority: run in parallel, ongoing, not blocking product proposals.
08 · Decision gates, risks, validation

When to continue, when to change course

Mid-term gate, month 3. If D1 and D7 retention of new cohorts clearly improve over baseline, and the day-0 check-in rate rises, continue.
Final gate, month 6. If real-user retention rises sustainably, double down. If not, revisit product positioning rather than fighting Chrome as a browser.

What must be true

  • Instrumentation is fixed, reliable enough to read the numbers.
  • Streamlined onboarding genuinely lifts day-0 activation.
  • Login-free earn is embraced by users without breaking from farming.
  • The core browsing value gets deepened.
Main risks. If instrumentation is not fixed, every conclusion is wrong. Opening anonymous earn can be farmed without caps and controls. Onboarding changes may lower the sign-in rate, so the offset must be measured via activation.

90-day validation

Run an A/B of streamlined onboarding and login-free earn on part of the user base. Measure day-0 activation, D1 and D7 retention, check-in rate, and merge rate after sign-in. Pass the gate when activation and retention clearly improve over the control group.

Glossary

Specialized terms used in this document

RetentionShare of users returning after a number of days.
ChurnUsers who leave and are no longer active in the period.
ActivationA new user completing a first value action (bookmark, download content, sign in).
OnboardingThe intro and setup screens on first app open.
CohortA group of users sharing a milestone, e.g. same first-open month, tracked over time.
MAU and DAUUsers active in 30 days (MAU) and in one day (DAU).
D1, D7, D30Return rate after 1, 7, 30 days from first open.
ResurrectedLong-dormant users coming back.
Right-censoredA cohort too young to be fully observed, so the number is understated.
FunnelA step-by-step conversion path, e.g. the wallet-creation flow.
UA (user acquisition)The work and budget to bring in new users.
Opt-inThe user actively turns on or agrees to participate.
CPACost per action, paid for each real user action.
